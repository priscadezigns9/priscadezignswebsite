How to Choose the Right Dash Cam for Your Car

Front-Only vs Dual Channel: What You Actually Need

A front-only dash cam covers the most common accident scenario — a collision in front of you. For most urban and suburban drivers, this is sufficient. However, rear-end collisions account for nearly 30% of all car accidents, and a dual-channel system with a rear camera gives you complete coverage. If you regularly drive in heavy traffic, park on busy streets, or use ride-share or delivery driving as income, a front-and-rear system is not optional — it is essential.

Dual-channel systems also catch incidents in car parks and petrol stations that occur at the rear of the vehicle. The cost premium over front-only cameras has dropped significantly — quality dual systems now start around $80 to $100 — making the full coverage choice easy to justify.

Resolution and Night Vision: Where Specs Matter

Resolution matters most in two specific scenarios: capturing number plates at distance and recording usable footage in low light. For plate capture, 1080p is the minimum — but at highway speeds, a 1440p or 4K front camera gives you more pixel density to zoom in post-incident without losing legibility. Night vision quality is determined by the image sensor, not the resolution spec. Cameras using Sony STARVIS or STARVIS 2 sensors perform dramatically better in low-light conditions than cheaper sensors at the same resolution rating.

Wide dynamic range (WDR) technology helps the camera handle high-contrast situations — like driving through a tunnel into bright sunlight — without overexposing or underexposing the image. Look for cameras that explicitly specify WDR or HDR support rather than vague marketing language about "night mode."

Power Source: Battery vs Capacitor vs Hardwire

Most dash cams are powered via your car's 12V socket or USB port. This is fine for basic recording while driving. For parking mode — where the camera continues recording while your car is parked and unattended — you need a hardwire kit that connects directly to your fuse box, drawing power even when the ignition is off.

Avoid dash cams with lithium-ion batteries in hot climates. Sustained temperatures above 60 degrees Celsius in a parked car degrade and can damage lithium batteries. Supercapacitor-based models handle heat far better and have a longer operational lifespan. If you live in a warm region or park in direct sun, a capacitor model with a hardwire kit is the combination to prioritize.

Storage and App Connectivity

Most dash cams use microSD cards in 32GB to 256GB capacities and record in loop mode — overwriting the oldest footage when the card is full. Buy a high-endurance microSD card rated for continuous write cycles; standard phone cards wear out quickly in this application. Wi-Fi connectivity allows you to pull footage directly to your smartphone app without removing the card. GPS logging records your speed and location on every frame, which becomes crucial evidence in accident disputes. For a buying decision summary: choose a dual-channel camera, prioritize sensor quality over raw resolution, use a capacitor model in hot climates, and always add a hardwire kit for parking mode.
