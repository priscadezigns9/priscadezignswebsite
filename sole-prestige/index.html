THE ICON

NIKE

“Just Do It” isn’t a slogan. It’s a philosophy worn by 400 million people. The Air Max changed cushioning forever. The Force changed basketball. The React changed running. Nike doesn’t make shoes — it makes moments.

Worn by champions who haven’t been crowned yet.

THE CULTURE

ADIDAS

Three stripes that built a culture. Born in a cobbler’s workshop in Bavaria. Worn on the feet of hip-hop’s greatest. The Stan Smith graced every school corridor. The Superstar every stage. The Ultraboost every morning run at 5am. Adidas doesn’t follow trends — it generates them.

Worn by those who show up, every single day.

THE LEGEND

AIR JORDAN

In 1984, Nike gave a rookie $2.5 million and a pair of shoes. The NBA tried to ban them. He wore them anyway — and paid the fine every single game. That rookie was Michael Jordan. Every Jordan release since has been less a shoe drop and more a cultural event.

Because some legacies are built one step at a time.

THE BOOT

TIMBERLAND

The Yellow Boot wasn’t designed for hip-hop. But hip-hop chose it — and that made it iconic. Built for construction sites, adopted by every subculture since. Worn on Notorious B.I.G.’s feet. Referenced in Destiny’s Child. Still going. Still relevant.

Built for the elements. Claimed by the culture.

THE ORIGINAL

CONVERSE

The Chuck Taylor All Star is over 100 years old. It’s still cool. That’s not luck. That’s design so pure it transcends trend. Artists, rebels, rockstars, students — everyone has owned a pair. Everyone remembers their first.

Everyone. Always.

THE ORIGINAL SKATER

VANS

Born in Anaheim, 1966. A tiny shop that let you design your own shoes the day they opened. Skaters adopted them because they gripped. Musicians adopted them because they were cheap. Now everyone wears them because they’re timeless.

Off the wall. Since day one.

THE CRAFT

The Greatest Shoes
Are Made By Hand.

Before machines. Before factories. Before mass production. There were craftsmen who spent lifetimes perfecting the art of the shoe. They still exist. And the shoes they make are extraordinary.

FLORENCE, ITALY

The Cordwainer’s Art

A cordwainer is a shoemaker who works only with new leather. The craft dates to the Middle Ages. In Florence, master cordwainers spend up to 40 hours on a single pair — hand-cutting, hand-stitching, hand-finishing. The result lasts decades.

NORTHAMPTON, ENGLAND

The Goodyear Welt

Invented in 1869. A strip of leather stitched between the upper and sole — creating a shoe that can be resoled indefinitely. A quality Goodyear-welted boot can outlast its owner. This is how Northampton built its reputation as the shoemaking capital of the world.

SAVILE ROW, LONDON

Bespoke — Made for One

A bespoke shoe begins with a wooden last carved to your exact foot shape — every contour, every measurement. The upper is cut, lasted, and stitched by a single craftsman. The process takes 6 months. The result fits no other foot on earth.

BEAVERTON, OREGON

The Air Unit

In 1977, an aerospace engineer named Frank Rudy walked into Nike with an idea: pressurised air inside a shoe. They almost said no. Today, Air is one of the most recognised design elements in the world. This is how it’s made.
