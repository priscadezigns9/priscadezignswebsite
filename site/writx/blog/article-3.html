Why Most Website Copy Fails (And the Fix Is Simpler Than You Think)

May 2026 · 6 min read

Most website copy fails not because of bad writing but because of bad thinking. The words are polished. The sentences flow. But when you read the whole thing, you have no idea who it is for, what problem it solves, or why you should care. The writing is fine. The strategy is absent.

The root cause

Most website copy is written from the inside out. The company starts with what they want to say about themselves and works outward. Good copy is written from the outside in. Start with your customer, their problem, and their language. Build everything from there.

Symptom 1: Features without benefits

A feature is what your product does. A benefit is what your customer gains. Most websites list features. Almost none of them translate those features into outcomes the customer actually cares about. For every feature you describe, ask: so what does this mean for the person reading this? That answer is your benefit. Lead with it.

Symptom 2: Generic claims

World-class, industry-leading, best-in-class, innovative. These phrases appear on millions of websites and mean nothing to anyone. Specificity builds credibility. Not we deliver fast but orders shipped within 4 hours. Not great customer support but average first-response time of 11 minutes. Numbers and specifics do the work that adjectives cannot.

Symptom 3: Writing for everyone

Copy that is written for everyone resonates with no one. The more specifically you write for your actual customer, the more powerfully it lands with them, and the more clearly it repels the wrong people. Both outcomes are good. Know your customer well enough to write for one person.

The simple fix

Before you write a single word, answer these three questions in writing: Who specifically is this for? What specific problem does it solve for them? Why should they believe you over any alternative? Every sentence you write should serve one of those three answers. Delete anything that does not.
