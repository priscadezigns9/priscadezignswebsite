How to Choose the Right Dash Cam

📅 2026 📖 5 min read ⭐ The Autodrome

A dash cam is one of the most practical safety investments you can make as a driver. In the event of an accident, fraudulent insurance claims, or road rage incidents, footage from your dash cam can be the difference between a resolved claim and a costly dispute. But with hundreds of models available at every price point, how do you choose the right one?

Video Resolution: 1080p Is the Minimum

Full HD 1080p should be your baseline. At this resolution, you'll capture number plates clearly enough to read in most lighting conditions. If your budget allows, step up to 1440p or 2K — the improvement in detail is noticeable, particularly at night or in low-contrast situations. 4K dash cams exist but come with significant storage demands.

Field of View

Look for a lens angle between 130° and 150°. Wider isn't always better — extremely wide-angle lenses distort the image at the edges, making it harder to read plates in peripheral frames. A 140° field of view strikes the right balance between coverage and clarity.

Night Vision and WDR

Most incidents happen at night or in challenging lighting. Wide Dynamic Range (WDR) technology balances bright and dark areas in the frame — essential for capturing detail when headlights or sunlight create extreme contrast. Sony STARVIS or similar low-light sensors are a mark of quality in this regard.

Loop Recording and Storage

All competent dash cams record in loops — once the memory card is full, the oldest footage is overwritten. Opt for a Class 10 or U3 microSD card rated for dash cam use. 32GB handles most needs; go 64GB or higher if you drive long distances regularly.

GPS Integration

A built-in GPS module stamps each recording with your speed and location. This can be invaluable if you need to prove where you were and how fast you were travelling during an incident — particularly useful in disputes about speed or positioning.

Parking Mode

Parking mode keeps the camera recording (or on motion-triggered standby) when the car is off. It's essential for capturing hit-and-run incidents in car parks. To use this feature continuously, you'll need a hardwire kit to draw power directly from your fuse box.

Dual-Channel: Front and Rear

A front-only camera leaves your rear exposed. Dual-channel systems record from both ends simultaneously — catching tailgaters, rear-end collisions and reversing incidents. If you can only afford one upgrade beyond the basics, make it a rear camera.

Build Quality and Mounting

Mount stability matters. A dash cam that vibrates or falls from your windscreen mid-journey is worse than useless. Suction mounts are easy to remove but can fail in heat. Adhesive mounts are more permanent and reliable. Ensure the unit sits within your vehicle's legal windscreen mounting zone.

Brand Reliability

Stick to established names with active firmware support — Vantrue, Garmin, BlackVue, Nextbase, and Thinkware all have strong reputations. Cheap unbranded units may function initially but often have poor night performance and no warranty support.

Take the time to read verified reviews and compare footage samples before buying. A dash cam is a long-term investment — choosing correctly the first time saves you the cost and frustration of replacing an inadequate unit.
