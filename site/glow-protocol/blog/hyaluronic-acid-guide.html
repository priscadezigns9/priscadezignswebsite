What is Hyaluronic Acid? The 2026 Guide

By Glow Protocol Editors · Published May 14, 2026

If you've spent any time researching skincare, you've seen hyaluronic acid (HA) everywhere — in serums, moisturisers, sheet masks, eye creams, and even lip balms. But what is it, how does it actually work, and does it live up to the hype? In 2026, hyaluronic acid remains one of the most researched and universally recommended skincare ingredients on the planet. This guide breaks it all down.

What is Hyaluronic Acid?

Hyaluronic acid is a naturally occurring polysaccharide (a type of sugar molecule) found in your skin, connective tissue, and joints. Its primary job in the body is to retain water — it can hold up to 1,000 times its own weight in moisture, making it one of the most powerful natural humectants known to science.

Your skin naturally produces hyaluronic acid, but production declines with age, sun exposure, and environmental stress. By your thirties, you have significantly less than in your teens — which contributes to dryness, loss of plumpness, and the appearance of fine lines. Applying it topically helps replenish what's lost.

Key fact: Hyaluronic acid is a humectant, not an emollient or occlusive. That means it draws moisture to the skin rather than adding oil or creating a barrier. It works best when there's moisture to attract — apply to damp skin for best results.

Molecular Weights Explained

Not all hyaluronic acid is the same. The size of the molecule (molecular weight) determines how deep it penetrates into the skin — and what it does when it gets there. In 2026, many serums use multi-weight HA blends for this reason.

High Molecular Weight HA

Larger molecules sit on the surface of the skin. They form a temporary moisture film that immediately reduces the appearance of fine lines and leaves skin looking plump and dewy. The effect is visible quickly — but it's largely cosmetic and doesn't penetrate deeply.

Low Molecular Weight HA

Smaller molecules penetrate further into the skin layers, providing deeper hydration and supporting the skin's own structure over time. These work more slowly but offer more lasting results. They're particularly valuable for mature or severely dehydrated skin.

What to Look For

The best hyaluronic acid products use multiple molecular weights — typically listed as "multi-weight hyaluronic acid," "sodium hyaluronate," or both. A good serum will address both surface hydration and deeper replenishment simultaneously.

How to Use Hyaluronic Acid Correctly

This is where many people go wrong. Hyaluronic acid draws moisture from its environment. If your environment (or your skin) is dry when you apply it, it can actually pull moisture from deeper skin layers, making dryness worse.

  1. Apply to damp skin. Pat your face dry but leave it slightly damp — not dripping. Then apply your HA serum immediately.
  2. Layer it under a moisturiser. Always follow HA with a cream or lotion to seal the moisture in. This is non-negotiable. Without an occlusive layer on top, the HA will evaporate.
  3. Use it morning and night. HA is gentle enough for twice-daily use and plays nicely with almost every other active.
  4. In dry climates or air-conditioned spaces, consider applying a facial mist after the HA and before your moisturiser to give it more moisture to draw from.

What Skin Types Should Use It?

Hyaluronic acid is genuinely one of the most universally suitable skincare ingredients available. Here's how it works across different skin types:

  • Dry skin: A game-changer. Provides immediate relief and long-term hydration support when used consistently.
  • Oily skin: Provides hydration without adding oil. Dehydrated oily skin is a real thing — over-production of sebum is often the skin compensating for lack of moisture.
  • Combination skin: Safe to use all over. Won't clog pores or create excess shine in the T-zone.
  • Sensitive or reactive skin: Hyaluronic acid is one of the gentlest actives available. It rarely causes reactions and is suitable for rosacea-prone skin.
  • Mature skin: Particularly effective at reducing the appearance of fine lines and restoring plumpness to areas that have lost volume over time.

Can You Use HA With Other Actives?

Yes — and this is one of its best qualities. Hyaluronic acid is non-reactive and plays well with almost everything in your routine:

  • Vitamin C: HA can help buffer potential irritation from L-ascorbic acid. Apply Vitamin C first, let it absorb, then apply HA.
  • Retinol: Using HA after retinol at night can significantly reduce the dryness and peeling associated with retinol use, especially when starting out.
  • AHA/BHA: Apply exfoliating acids first, allow them to work for a few minutes, then layer HA on top to restore hydration.
  • Niacinamide: One of the most popular combinations. Both improve skin texture and barrier function — use together without any issues.

How Long Until You See Results?

Surface hydration and plumpness are visible almost immediately — within an hour of application, most people notice their skin looks more dewy and any fine lines appear softer. For deeper hydration and longer-term barrier improvement, expect consistent results from 4–6 weeks of twice-daily use.

The Bottom Line

Hyaluronic acid is not a trend. It's a clinically validated, universally compatible ingredient that belongs in almost every skincare routine. Apply it to damp skin, layer it under a moisturiser, and use it consistently — and it will deliver. Whether you're 22 or 62, oily or dry, the right HA serum is one of the easiest wins in modern skincare.
