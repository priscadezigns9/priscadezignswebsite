Vitamin C vs Retinol - Which Should You Use?

Vitamin C and retinol are the two most frequently recommended skincare actives. They both work. They address different concerns. And how you use them matters as much as whether you use them.

What Vitamin C Does

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid and its derivatives) is a potent antioxidant that neutralises free radical damage from UV and pollution. It also inhibits melanin production, making it effective for brightening and fading hyperpigmentation. Vitamin C is a morning ingredient — it works synergistically with SPF to boost photoprotection.

What Retinol Does

Retinol (a form of Vitamin A) increases cell turnover, stimulates collagen production, and regulates sebum. Over time it visibly reduces fine lines, improves texture, and clears congestion. It takes 12-16 weeks of consistent use to see significant results. Retinol is a night ingredient — it degrades in UV exposure and increases photosensitivity.

Can You Use Both?

Yes — but not at the same time. The standard protocol: Vitamin C in the morning (after cleansing, before moisturiser and SPF), retinol at night (after cleansing, before moisturiser). They do not interact negatively when used at different times of day.

Which to Start With

Start with one. If your primary concern is dullness, uneven skin tone, or sun damage, start with Vitamin C. If your primary concern is texture, fine lines, or acne, start with retinol. Give whichever you choose 12 weeks of consistent use before evaluating results.

Vitamin C Stability

L-ascorbic acid (the most effective form) is unstable — it oxidises on exposure to light and air. Buy it in a dark, opaque bottle. If your Vitamin C serum turns orange or brown, it has oxidised and should be replaced. Vitamin C derivatives (ascorbyl glucoside, sodium ascorbyl phosphate) are more stable and gentler, though slightly less potent.

Retinol Sensitivity

Start low (0.025-0.05%), use twice weekly, and build slowly over months. The "skin cycling" method (one night retinol, one night exfoliant, two nights recovery) reduces initial sensitivity while maintaining efficacy.
