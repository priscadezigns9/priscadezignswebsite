GPS Pet Trackers — Are They Worth It in 2026?

GPS pet trackers attach to your pet's collar and let you see their location in real time on your phone. The technology has matured significantly — but the value depends on your situation. Here's an honest assessment.

How They Work

GPS trackers use cellular networks (not just GPS) to transmit location data to your phone. This means they require a monthly subscription (typically $5-15/month) in addition to the device cost. Some use Bluetooth for short-range tracking only — these are significantly cheaper but useless if your pet leaves the neighbourhood.

Accuracy

Most quality GPS trackers are accurate to 3-10 metres in open areas. In urban environments with GPS interference, accuracy can drop to 20-30 metres. This is close enough to pinpoint a garden or street — but not an exact room inside a building.

Battery Life

The biggest limitation. Live tracking drains batteries fast — expect 1-3 days on active tracking, 7-14 days on standard mode. If you forget to charge it, it's useless when you need it most. USB-C charging and low-battery alerts are essential features.

Size and Weight

For small cats and small dog breeds, weight matters significantly. Trackers over 30g are uncomfortable on animals under 5kg. The best trackers for small pets are under 15-20g.

Who Actually Needs One

  • Cats that go outdoors regularly
  • Dogs that escape fences or bolt on walks
  • Pet owners in rural areas where a lost pet could travel far
  • Anyone whose pet has gone missing before

Who Probably Doesn't

  • Indoor-only cats
  • Dogs always walked on lead in urban areas
  • If the ongoing subscription cost isn't practical
