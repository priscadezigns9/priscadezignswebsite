Cat Enrichment 101:
Keep Your Cat Happy and Stimulated

Published May 16, 2026 By the Paw Vault Team

Indoor cats live longer than outdoor cats — by an average of 10–15 years. But longevity without quality of life is a welfare concern, not a success story. A bored indoor cat will develop behavioural problems: overgrooming, aggression, destructive scratching, anxiety, and obesity. The solution is enrichment.

Enrichment isn't a luxury add-on for cat owners who have too much money. It's the baseline requirement for a mentally healthy indoor cat. Here's how to do it properly.

What Is Cat Enrichment?

Enrichment means providing environmental complexity and stimulation that satisfies a cat's natural behavioural needs. Domestic cats are descended from solitary hunters. They're wired to hunt, stalk, pounce, climb, scratch, and patrol a territory. An apartment with a food bowl and a bed satisfies none of these drives.

Vet insight: "The majority of behavioural problems I see in indoor cats — overgrooming, aggression, soiling outside the litter box — have an enrichment deficit as a root cause. Enrichment isn't optional, it's therapeutic." — Dr. A. Richards, DVM

Environmental Enrichment: Space & Structure

🏰
Vertical Space

Cat Tree + Wall-Mounted Shelves

Cats are vertical animals. Giving your cat height — a multi-level cat tree, wall-mounted shelves, or a window perch — dramatically increases their perceived territory size and provides a safe vantage point. A tall cat tree near a window is the single highest-ROI enrichment purchase.

Sensory · Nature Access

Cat Grass & Catnip Garden (Indoor)

Wheatgrass, cat grass, and catnip give indoor cats sensory access to nature they'd otherwise never experience. Cat grass also helps with digestion and hairball management. A small grow-your-own kit sits on a windowsill and takes under a week to sprout.

Scratching · Territory Marking

Horizontal & Vertical Scratching Posts

Scratching isn't bad behaviour — it's necessary behaviour. Cats scratch to maintain their claws, stretch their spine, and mark territory. If you don't provide appropriate surfaces, they'll use your furniture. Get both horizontal (corrugated cardboard) and vertical (sisal rope) options.

Feeding Enrichment: Make Them Work for Food

In the wild, cats hunt 10–20 times a day. Serving food in a static bowl takes 2 minutes and satisfies none of the hunting drive. Feeding enrichment changes this.

🧩
Puzzle Feeder · Mental Stimulation

Rotating Puzzle Feeder (3-Layer)

Multi-layer feeders hide kibble in compartments that rotate, lift, and slide. A cat who eats from a puzzle feeder is engaged for 10–20 minutes per meal instead of 2. This slows eating, reduces overeating, and provides significant daily mental stimulation.

Foraging · Instinct-Based

Foraging Balls & Kibble Dispensers

A simple plastic ball with holes that dispenses kibble as the cat bats it around. Basic, cheap, and genuinely effective for encouraging movement and engaging prey-drive behaviour during mealtimes. Rotate the size and style to keep it novel.

Play Enrichment: Interactive Toys

🎣
Play · Interactive · Daily

Wand Toys (Feather & Ribbon)

15 minutes of interactive wand play per day is the most important thing you can do for your cat's mental health. The chase-and-catch cycle satisfies the hunt sequence completely — stalk, chase, pounce, catch, kill. Always end sessions by letting your cat "catch" the prey so they feel fulfilled, not frustrated.

For the hours you can't be there, automated toys with motion sensors or randomised movement patterns keep cats engaged independently. A Simple Enrichment Routine

You don't need to overhaul your home. Start with this daily routine:

  • Morning: Use a puzzle feeder for breakfast. Refill the cat grass if empty.
  • Midday: Leave a foraging ball or lick mat out with a small amount of food.
  • Evening: 15 minutes of interactive wand play. Non-negotiable.
  • Bedtime: Puzzle feeder for dinner, or scatter feeding in a snuffle mat.
  • Weekly: Rotate which toys are accessible. Retire old ones temporarily so they feel new when they return.

This routine takes less than 20 active minutes per day and will measurably reduce stress behaviours, improve fitness, and deepen your bond with your cat.
