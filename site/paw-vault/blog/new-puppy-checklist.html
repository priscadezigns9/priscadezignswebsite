New Puppy Checklist: Everything You Need in Week One

May 2026 8 min read Puppy Guide

Bringing a new puppy home is one of the most exciting — and chaotic — experiences a pet owner can have. The first week sets the foundation for your dog's behavior, health habits, and your relationship. Being prepared means fewer emergency trips to the pet store at midnight, fewer accidents from inadequate confinement, and a puppy that feels safe and settled from day one. This checklist covers everything you need, organized by category, with honest guidance on what's essential versus what's nice-to-have.

Before They Arrive: The Non-Negotiables

These are the items that should be in your home before your puppy arrives. A puppy arriving to an unprepared home is a recipe for stress — for both of you. Set up these items at least 24 hours in advance.

🏠 Shelter & Confinement

  • Crate (appropriately sized): Large enough for adult size, divider included to keep it cozy now. The crate is your puppy's den — introduce it positively from day one.
  • Playpen / exercise pen: For supervised free time outside the crate without giving run of the entire house.
  • Baby gates: Block off stairs, kitchens, or rooms until the puppy is trained.
  • Puppy bed or crate mat: Washable, chew-resistant. Avoid expensive beds until chewing phase is over.

🍽️ Feeding Essentials

  • Food and water bowls: Ceramic or stainless steel — both are easier to sanitize than plastic, and plastic can harbor bacteria. Ceramic bowls are heavier and less likely to slide during enthusiastic eating.
  • Puppy-appropriate food: Ask your breeder/shelter what food the puppy has been eating. Switch gradually to avoid GI upset if changing brands.
  • Food storage container: Keeps food fresh and pest-free. Airtight seal matters.
  • Slow feeder bowl (optional): Reduces eating speed, helps prevent bloat in fast eaters.

🦮 Walking & Safety

  • Collar with ID tag: Fitted properly — two fingers should slide under comfortably. ID tag with your phone number is essential from day one.
  • 6-foot leash: Standard length for walking and training. Not a retractable leash — those teach dogs to pull and provide no control.
  • Harness (optional but recommended): Reduces neck strain for puppies who pull. Front-clip harnesses reduce pulling better than back-clip.
  • Microchipping: Schedule within the first week. More reliable than a collar tag if your puppy escapes.

🧸 Enrichment & Training

  • Chew toys: Multiple textures — rubber, rope, antler. Puppies need to chew; give them appropriate outlets to save your furniture.
  • Interactive toys: Kongs, puzzle feeders, snuffle mats. Mental stimulation tires puppies as much as physical exercise.
  • Training treats: Small, soft, high-value. You'll use hundreds in the first week. Cut them small — thumbnail-sized pieces.
  • Puppy blanket: A soft, washable blanket for the crate that carries your scent. Comfort is key for settling a new puppy overnight.

🏥 Health & Grooming

  • Vet appointment booked: Within 48–72 hours of arrival. Your vet will confirm vaccination status, check for parasites, and become your first point of contact for health questions.
  • Puppy shampoo: Formulated for puppies (adult formulas can irritate sensitive skin).
  • Nail clippers or grinder: Start handling paws immediately so nail care becomes routine. Puppy nails grow fast and get sharp.
  • Enzyme cleaner: Non-negotiable for the first weeks. Eliminates the odor compounds that attract puppies to repeat accidents in the same spot. Standard cleaners don't work.
  • Poop bags: Buy in bulk. You'll use many more than you expect.

The First Night: What to Expect

Most puppies cry the first night away from their littermates and mother. This is normal, expected, and temporary. Place the crate near your bed so the puppy can hear and smell you. Some owners place a warm water bottle wrapped in a towel (simulating littermate warmth) inside the crate. A piece of clothing you've worn can also provide comfort through scent.

Do not give in to crying by taking the puppy out of the crate — this teaches the puppy that crying works. If you must respond, calmly check they haven't had an accident, then return them and leave. Within 3–5 nights, most puppies have adjusted significantly. By week two, many sleep through the night without incident.

Week One Schedule Framework

Puppies thrive on routine. A basic schedule: out immediately after waking, 10–15 minutes after eating, after play, and before bed. Puppies can generally hold their bladder one hour per month of age — a 2-month-old puppy can hold it for approximately 2 hours. Plan accordingly.

Training should start day one with simple behaviors: sit, name response, and crate entry. Use treats liberally. Short sessions of 3–5 minutes are more effective than longer ones. The puppy brain learns fastest through positive reinforcement during brief, frequent repetitions.
