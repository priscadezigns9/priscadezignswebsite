How to Propagate Plants: 5 Easy Methods

Propagation is the art of growing new plants from pieces of existing ones — and it's one of the most satisfying skills a plant parent can develop. Here are the 5 most effective methods, with step-by-step instructions for each.

Method 1: Water Propagation

Best for: Pothos, Philodendron, Tradescantia, Impatiens. Cut a stem just below a node (the little bump where leaves emerge). Remove leaves from the bottom 2 inches. Place in a glass of room-temperature water, ensuring the node is submerged but leaves stay dry. Change water weekly. Roots appear in 2-4 weeks. Pot up once roots are 2 inches long.

Method 2: Soil Propagation

Best for: Succulents, Rosemary, Lavender, most herbs. Take a 4-6 inch cutting, remove lower leaves, dip the cut end in rooting hormone powder, and plant in moist propagation mix. Cover with a plastic dome or bag to retain humidity. Keep warm and bright but out of direct sun. Check for root resistance (gently tug) after 3-4 weeks.

Method 3: Leaf Propagation

Best for: Succulents, Begonias, African Violets, Snake Plants. Gently twist or cut a healthy leaf with the petiole (stem) intact. Let the cut end callous for 24 hours, then place on top of moist succulent soil. New plantlets emerge from the base in 4-8 weeks. Don't water until you see growth — leaves store all the moisture they need.

Methods 4 & 5: Division and Offsets

Division is ideal for plants that grow in clumps like Peace Lilies, Hostas, and Boston Ferns. Simply unpot the parent plant and gently separate the root mass into two or more sections, ensuring each has roots and leaves. Offsets (pups) are baby plants that some species — aloes, bromeliads, agaves — produce naturally at the base. Simply wait until the pup is one-third the size of the parent, then slice it away and pot it up separately.
