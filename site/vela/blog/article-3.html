How to Design a Landing Page That Actually Converts

May 2026 · 6 min read

A landing page has one job: to convert a visitor into a lead, a customer, or a subscriber. Everything on the page should serve that one goal. Everything that does not serve it should not be on the page.

Start with the offer, not the design

The most common landing page mistake is starting with the design. Design should serve the offer. Before you open any design tool, write out what you are offering, who it is for, what problem it solves, and why someone should believe you. The design is just the presentation of those answers.

The above-the-fold equation

Above the fold means what visitors see before they scroll. It needs to communicate: what this is, who it is for, and what happens when they take action. A strong headline, a supporting subheadline, and a clear call-to-action button. That is the minimum viable above-the-fold.

Build trust systematically

Visitors who do not trust you will not convert, no matter how good your offer is. Social proof, credentials, testimonials, guarantees, and transparent pricing all contribute to trust. Place them strategically where objections are most likely to arise, not just at the bottom of the page.

One page, one action

Remove navigation. Remove links to other pages. Remove anything that takes the visitor away from the single action you want them to take. Every exit you close is a conversion you keep.

Test everything

Your intuition about what will convert is a hypothesis. The page itself is the experiment. A/B test your headline, your CTA copy, your button colour. Small changes often produce large results. The data will tell you what your intuition could not.
