7 Web Design Mistakes That Are Killing Your Conversions

May 2026 · 6 min read

Most websites do not have a traffic problem. They have a conversion problem. Visitors arrive, look around, and leave without taking the action the business needs. In almost every case, the cause is one of the same seven mistakes.

1. No clear hero statement

Your visitor should know within three seconds what you do, who it is for, and why they should care. Most heroes lead with a clever tagline that communicates nothing. Replace it with a direct, specific statement of value. Specificity always converts better than cleverness.

2. Too many calls to action

When you ask people to do everything, they do nothing. Every page should have one primary call to action. Secondary actions should be clearly subordinate. Decision paralysis is real and it costs you conversions every single day.

3. Slow load times

Every second of load time above two seconds costs you a measurable percentage of visitors. Most slow sites are not slow because of server problems. They are slow because of oversized images, render-blocking scripts, and fonts loaded inefficiently. Fix the basics before you redesign anything.

4. No social proof above the fold

Trust is earned, not assumed. Testimonials, logos of companies you have worked with, review ratings, and case study snippets should appear early in the page, not buried at the bottom after you have already lost the visitor.

5. Forms that ask too much

Every additional form field reduces completion rate. Ask for the minimum information you actually need at this stage of the relationship. You can collect more data later. Right now, your job is to get the conversion.

6. No mobile optimisation

More than half of web traffic is mobile. A site that works beautifully on desktop but breaks on a phone is a site that rejects the majority of its visitors. Test every page on real devices, not just browser simulations.

7. Unclear visual hierarchy

Good design guides the eye. If everything on your page is bold, nothing is bold. Typography scale, white space, and contrast should all work together to direct attention to what matters most, in the order it matters.
