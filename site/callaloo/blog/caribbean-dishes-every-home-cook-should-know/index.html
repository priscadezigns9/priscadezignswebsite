10 Caribbean Dishes Every Home Cook Should Know How to Make

These are not just recipes — they are the foundation of Caribbean food culture. Know these dishes and you understand the kitchen. Master them and you never need a takeaway again.

Caribbean cooking has a vast repertoire, but certain dishes appear across households, across islands, and across generations. They are the ones people ask for at family gatherings, the ones that bring people back home regardless of where they have ended up. Every serious home cook should know these.

1. Pelau

The one-pot wonder of Caribbean cooking. Rice, pigeon peas, and protein (almost always chicken) cooked together with caramelised sugar, coconut milk, and whatever vegetables are in the kitchen. Once you understand the technique — brown the meat in burnt sugar first, then build everything on top — you can make pelau without a recipe. It lives in your hands.

2. Callaloo

Dasheen bush or amaranth leaves cooked down with okra, coconut milk, and seasoning into a thick, intensely flavoured side dish. It pairs with everything. It freezes well. And the nutritional value is extraordinary — iron, calcium, vitamins A and C, all in one pot.

3. Stew Chicken

Chicken marinated in green seasoning, browned in burnt sugar, and stewed low and slow. The browning step — getting the sugar to exactly the right stage before adding the chicken — is the skill. Get that right and the rest follows. This is the foundational technique behind half of Caribbean cooking.

"Caribbean cooking is built on instinct, smell, and taste. The best cooks measure nothing and get it right every time — because they understand the technique, not just the recipe."

4. Doubles

Two pieces of fried bara (soft, turmeric-seasoned fried dough) with curried channa and a range of condiments — tamarind sauce, pepper sauce, chadon beni, cucumber. Doubles is street food, but making it at home is entirely achievable once you crack the bara texture. The channa can be made in advance and keeps well.

5. Curry (Chicken, Shrimp, or Vegetable)

Caribbean curry is distinct from Indian and Southeast Asian curries — built on a specific spice blend and technique, with chadon beni and shadow beni in the seasoning. The curry paste must be cooked in oil until fragrant before anything else goes in. Rushing that step is the most common mistake.

6. Provisions

Boiled root vegetables — dasheen, yam, cassava, sweet potato, green fig, eddoe — served with butter or oil and salt. Simple, filling, nutritious, and the foundation of almost every traditional meal. Knowing how to prepare provisions properly (each vegetable has a different cooking time) is basic competence in a Caribbean kitchen.

7. Bake

Fried bake (soft, pillowy fried dough) or baked bake (denser, bread-like). The perfect vehicle for everything — saltfish buljol, smoked herring, cheese, avocado. Bake-making is a skill that takes a few attempts to get right but once you have the dough texture correct, it becomes second nature.

8. Saltfish Buljol

Desalted and flaked saltfish mixed with tomatoes, onion, sweet pepper, and chadon beni. Served with bake, bread, or as a side. The desalting process matters — changing the water several times over a few hours, or boiling and refreshing. Under-desalted buljol is inedible. Properly done, it is exceptional.

9. Oil Down (Breadfruit)

The national dish of Grenada and a staple across the islands. Breadfruit, meat, and vegetables cooked down in coconut milk seasoned with turmeric and various herbs until the liquid is fully absorbed and only the oil from the coconut milk remains. It takes time but requires almost no active cooking once everything is in the pot.

10. Black Cake

The Christmas cake. Dried fruits soaked in rum and cherry brandy for weeks — sometimes months — then folded into a dark, dense, intensely flavoured cake. Black cake is a project, not a recipe. It requires planning. But making it yourself, from fruits you soaked yourself, is one of the most satisfying things you can do in a Caribbean kitchen.
