How to Track Calories When You Eat Local Food (Without Giving Up the Food You Love)

Standard nutrition apps were not built for people who eat rice and peas, stew chicken, bake and shark, or doubles. Here is how to track your food honestly without having to pretend you eat the same things as someone in Ohio.

Calorie tracking has a representation problem. The apps that dominate the market — MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, Lose It — are built around food systems where a "typical meal" is a grilled chicken breast with broccoli or a slice of avocado toast. If you eat Caribbean food regularly, you are either searching endlessly for approximations, giving up on tracking entirely, or — worse — logging something inaccurate and making decisions based on bad data.

None of those are good options. Here is a better approach.

Understand what you are actually trying to track

Calorie tracking is useful as a directional tool, not a precise science. Even the best nutrition databases have margins of error. What matters is consistency and relative accuracy — being roughly right every day is far more valuable than being precisely right about one meal and ignoring the rest.

With that in mind, the goal when eating local food is to build a personal database of your regular meals with as much accuracy as possible, then use that consistently.

Start with your most-eaten meals:

Most people eat 10–15 meals on rotation. Identify yours. Accurately estimate those meals once, save them, and you have covered 80% of your diet. You do not need to track every obscure dish — start with what you actually eat most.

How to estimate local meals without a database

When a dish is not in the app, break it into components. A plate of pelau is rice + pigeon peas + chicken + seasonings + a small amount of oil. Each of those exists in databases. Estimate your portion size, log each component, and you have a reasonable approximation.

It sounds more tedious than it is. After you do it once for a dish, save it as a custom meal. You will never have to do it again for that dish.

Portion estimation is the real skill

Whether you eat local food or not, the main source of tracking error is portion size — not database gaps. Most people consistently underestimate portions by 20–40%. Learning to estimate by visual cues (a cup of rice looks like a tennis ball; a palm-sized piece of protein is roughly 3–4oz) closes more of the gap than finding a perfect database entry.

"The person who estimates portions consistently and roughly correctly will always have more useful data than the person who tracks precisely only when they eat 'trackable' food."
