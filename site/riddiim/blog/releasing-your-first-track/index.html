7 Tips for Releasing Your First Track

May 27, 2026 • 6 min read

So, you’ve finally finished that track. The mix is tight, the master is loud, and you're ready to show the world what you've been working on in your bedroom studio for the last six months. But wait—don't hit upload just yet. Releasing your first track is about more than just clicking 'submit' on a distributor's dashboard. It's about building a moment.

1. The 4-Week Lead Time

Give yourself at least four weeks before your release date. This isn't just for the distribution services to process your files; it's for pitching. If you want a chance at editorial playlists on Spotify or Apple Music, you need to submit your track via their artist portals at least three weeks in advance.

2. High-Fidelity Visuals

In a scrolling world, people see your music before they hear it. Your cover art shouldn't look like an afterthought. High-resolution, professional visuals that reflect the vibe of the track are non-negotiable. If you're not a designer, invest in one. It pays off.

3. Claim Your Artist Profiles

Before the track drops, make sure you have claimed your profiles on Spotify for Artists and Apple Music for Artists. This gives you access to the data and allows you to customize your bio, header images, and "Artist Pick" sections.

4. The Tease Campaign

Start posting snippets of the track two weeks out. Use TikTok and Reels to showcase the "hook" or the most energetic part of the song. Don't just post the audio—show the process, the studio vibes, or even just you vibing to it. People connect with the artist as much as the art.

5. Smart Links are Your Best Friend

Use a service like Linktree or ToneDen to create a pre-save link. This allows fans to "save" your track to their library before it's even out, which signals to the algorithms that there is genuine hype on release day.

6. Plan Your "Next"

Your first track shouldn't be your last for six months. The best way to build momentum is consistency. Have the second track finished and ready to go before the first one even drops. The goal is to keep the listeners you've just gained.

7. Engage, Don't Just Broadcast

When the track drops, reply to every comment. Share every story that tags your music. Building a fanbase is a two-way street. Your first 100 fans are the most important ones you'll ever have—treat them like it.
