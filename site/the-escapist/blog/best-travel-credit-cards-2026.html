Best Travel Credit Cards 2026: Maximize Your Points

Published May 23, 2026  ·  11 min read  ·  Travel Finance

The right travel credit card can fund an entire international trip through sign-up bonuses alone — and the ongoing rewards stack keeps paying for years. In 2026, the competition for your wallet is fiercer than ever, and issuers are packing in more perks. Here's how to choose the card that fits your travel style.

Travel credit cards work on a simple but powerful principle: you earn points or miles on everyday spending, then redeem them for flights, hotels, and experiences at a value far above face value. The best cards also layer on statement credits, lounge access, insurance protections, and transfer partners that let you move points to airlines and hotels at favourable rates.

How to Maximize Your Travel Points in 2026

Stack a premium and a no-fee card: Use the Sapphire Reserve for travel and dining (3x), and pair it with a flat 2% cash-back card for everything else. Or use the Amex Platinum for flights (5x) and add a no-fee card for groceries. Two-card strategies routinely outperform single-card solutions.

Hit the sign-up bonus every time: The sign-up bonus is always the biggest single source of value from any travel card. Coordinate new card applications with planned large purchases (home renovations, annual insurance premiums, quarterly tax payments) to hit minimum spends without changing your normal habits.

Transfer to airline partners, never cash out: Points cashed out as statement credits typically return 1 cent per point. The same points transferred to an airline partner like United or British Airways can return 5–10 cents per point on premium cabin redemptions. The multiplier is the entire game.

2026 Trend: Airline co-brand cards are fighting back. Delta's Amex cards, United's Chase cards, and Alaska's BofA cards have all added elevated sign-up bonuses and expanded transfer partnerships in 2026. If you have a home airport hub with one dominant airline, the co-brand card stack can beat a general travel card — especially for status qualification.

What to Watch Out For

Devaluation risk: Award programs periodically devalue their points — increasing the number needed for the same redemption. Avoid hoarding large point balances indefinitely; redeem within 18–24 months of earning.

Foreign transaction fees: Any travel card worth carrying should waive foreign transaction fees entirely. If yours charges 1–3% on international purchases, replace it before your next trip abroad.

Travel insurance fine print: Premium travel cards include trip cancellation, interruption, and delay coverage — but only when you pay for the trip with that card. Read the terms carefully. Some policies require you pay the full fare on the card; others only require partial payment.
