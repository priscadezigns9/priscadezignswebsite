How to Shop for Quality Over Quantity

Most people know they should buy less and buy better. Few actually do it. The obstacle is not knowledge — it is the absence of a practical framework for making decisions in the moment. Here is one that works.

The Cost-Per-Wear Calculation

Divide the price of an item by the number of times you expect to wear it. A $300 cashmere jumper worn 150 times costs $2 per wear. A $40 polyester top worn 4 times costs $10 per wear. The expensive item is cheaper in practice. Run this calculation before every purchase.

The 30-Wear Rule

Before buying any clothing item, ask: will I wear this at least 30 times? If the answer is uncertain, don't buy it. Most impulse purchases fail this test immediately.

The One-In-One-Out Rule

Every new item coming in means one existing item going out. This disciplines purchasing decisions and forces you to evaluate what you actually value. If nothing in your wardrobe deserves to leave to make room for the new item, the new item probably doesn't deserve to enter.

Touch the Fabric

Natural fibres (wool, cashmere, linen, silk, cotton) breathe, regulate temperature, and age well. Synthetics pill, retain odour, and degrade. Touch is the fastest quality indicator. If it feels cheap, it is cheap.

Check the Construction

Turn the garment inside out. Look at the seam allowances (wider is better), the stitching (consistent spacing, no loose threads), and the lining if present. The inside of a garment tells you more about its quality than the outside does.

Buy the Best Version You Can Afford

Within any category, there is a point of diminishing returns. The goal is to identify the best version you can afford of each essential and buy that — not the cheapest, not the most expensive, but the one that maximises value within your actual budget.
