How to Clean Sneakers: The Complete Guide

May 2026 8 min read Sneaker Care

A great sneaker deserves great care. Whether you're protecting a grail pair for resale value or simply extending the life of your daily rotation, cleaning technique matters — and it varies significantly by material. Getting it wrong can discolor leather, damage suede irreparably, or shrink canvas beyond wearability. This guide covers the right approach for every major sneaker material, the tools that make a genuine difference, and the habits that keep your collection looking fresh indefinitely.

Before You Start: The Universal Rules

Regardless of material, certain principles apply to every cleaning session:

  • Remove laces before cleaning — clean separately in a bowl with warm soapy water, or replace entirely
  • Remove insoles — clean separately, air dry completely before reinserting
  • Never machine wash — the agitation cycle damages adhesives, soles, and upper materials on virtually every sneaker
  • Never use heat to dry — dryers, radiators, and direct sunlight all yellow midsoles, crack leather, and distort foam cushioning
  • Always stuff with white paper or cedar shoe trees during drying — maintains shape and absorbs moisture from inside
  • Test any product on a hidden area first — different dye lots react differently to cleaning solutions

Leather — Jordan 1, Air Force 1, Stan Smith

  • Tools needed: Soft-bristle brush, microfiber cloth, mild soap or dedicated leather cleaner
  • Mix a few drops of dish soap with warm water. Dip the brush (soft bristles only — medium or hard bristles scratch leather surfaces), then scrub the upper in gentle circular motions.
  • Wipe with a clean damp cloth. Never saturate leather — moisture can stain and distort the material.
  • For stubborn scuffs, a small amount of white eraser or dedicated leather eraser removes surface marks effectively.
  • After cleaning, apply a leather conditioner to restore moisture — leather that dries out cracks over time. Crep Protect, Jason Markk, or even standard leather conditioner works well.
  • Finish with a water-repellent spray. This is the single best preventative step against future staining.

Mesh and Knit — Nike React, Yeezy 350, Ultra Boost

  • Tools needed: Soft-bristle brush, microfiber cloth, mild soap
  • Mesh is porous — dirt penetrates deeper than on leather. Use a soft brush to first agitate and loosen dirt from the fibers before introducing liquid.
  • Work in the same mild soap solution, brush gently in the direction of the mesh weave rather than against it.
  • Rinse with a cloth dampened with clean water — don't submerge the shoe.
  • Air dry stuffed with white paper. Mesh dries quickly but make sure the inside is fully dry before wearing to prevent bacterial growth.
  • For yellowed white mesh: a diluted hydrogen peroxide solution (3% pharmacy peroxide mixed 1:1 with warm water) can restore brightness when applied with a brush, then left in indirect sunlight for 20–30 minutes. Test first.

Suede — New Balance 574, Vans Old Skool Suede, Jordan 1 Premium

  • Tools needed: Suede brush, suede eraser, suede protector spray
  • Suede is the most delicate sneaker material and the easiest to damage permanently. Never use water on dry suede stains — it creates a watermark ring that's difficult to remove.
  • For dry dirt: use a suede brush in one direction to lift dirt from the nap. Then use the brush's rubber edge or a suede eraser on stubborn marks.
  • For wet stains: blot immediately with a clean cloth (never rub), allow to dry completely, then brush to restore the nap.
  • For deep stains: white vinegar on a cloth, applied sparingly, can lift many stains from suede without the watermark. Test on a hidden area first.
  • After every cleaning: apply suede protector spray. This step is non-negotiable for suede — it creates a barrier against future moisture and staining that makes all subsequent cleaning dramatically easier.

Canvas — Converse Chuck Taylor, Vans Old Skool

  • Tools needed: Medium-bristle brush, toothbrush for details, mild soap or dish soap
  • Canvas is the most forgiving material to clean. It tolerates more moisture and agitation than leather or suede.
  • Create a soap solution and scrub with a medium bristle brush in circular motions. Canvas can handle more pressure than other materials.
  • For white canvas that has yellowed: baking soda mixed with a small amount of water creates a paste that when applied with a toothbrush and left for 15 minutes, then scrubbed off, effectively whitens canvas.
  • Rinse thoroughly with a damp cloth. Canvas holds soap well, so rinsing is important to prevent residue.
  • Air dry away from direct heat. Canvas typically dries within a few hours.

Midsole Cleaning: The Often-Missed Step

Dirty midsoles drag down the look of even clean uppers. Most midsole material (EVA foam, rubber) responds well to a Magic Eraser (melamine foam) — dampen it slightly and scrub the midsole in short, controlled strokes. For deep yellowing on vintage pairs, the hydrogen peroxide method combined with UV exposure (sunlight or a UV lamp) produces dramatic whitening results on EVA foam.

Sole edges and outsoles can be cleaned more aggressively — an old toothbrush with dish soap removes embedded dirt from grooves effectively. A cotton swab dipped in acetone handles black scuff marks on white rubber edges, but use it precisely — acetone can affect surrounding materials.
