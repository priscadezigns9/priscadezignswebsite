Collector's Glossary

Movement Types

Everything you need to speak watch. Understand the mechanics behind every masterpiece.

⚙

Automatic

Self-winding via a rotor that rotates with the wearer's movement. No battery required. The most popular choice in fine watchmaking.

🔩

Manual Wind

Hand-wound via the crown. A traditional, intimate ritual. Favoured in ultra-thin dress watches and grand complications.

⚡

Quartz

Battery-powered and accurate to plus or minus 15 seconds per month. Dominated the 1970s Quartz Crisis and still leads in precision sport models.

🌀

Tourbillon

A rotating cage that counteracts gravity's effect on the escapement. Invented by Abraham-Louis Breguet in 1801. Pure horological theatre.

📅

Perpetual Calendar

Automatically adjusts for short months and leap years. A mechanical masterpiece that won't need manual correction until 2100.

⏱

Chronograph

A stopwatch complication integrated into the timepiece. From column-wheel to vertical clutch — the complexity defines the price.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best entry-level luxury watch?

The Seiko Presage, Tissot PRX, and Hamilton Khaki Field offer Swiss/Japanese craftsmanship under $500. For first luxury purchases, the Tudor Black Bay is the benchmark under $4,000.

Do watches hold their value?

Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet consistently appreciate. Sports models (Submariner, Daytona, Nautilus) outperform the broader luxury market. Quartz watches generally do not.

What is the difference between automatic and quartz?

Automatic movements are wound by wrist motion — no battery, rich heritage, slight drift (~5 sec/day). Quartz uses a battery-powered crystal oscillator — extremely accurate to 15 sec/month.

How do I wind an automatic watch?

If fully unwound, manually wind by rotating the crown clockwise 20–30 times. Once running, normal wrist movement maintains power reserve. Do not over-wind.

What watches are the best investments in 2026?

The Rolex Submariner, Patek Philippe Calatrava, AP Royal Oak, and Tudor Black Bay continue to dominate the secondary market. Micro-brands like Ming and Baltic are emerging investment plays.

What makes Rolex so expensive?

Vertically integrated manufacturing (Rolex makes their own steel, movements, and bracelets), brutal quality control, controlled supply to authorised dealers, and 120 years of cultural prestige.
