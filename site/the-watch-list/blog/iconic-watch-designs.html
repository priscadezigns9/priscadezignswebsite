The 10 Most Iconic Watch Designs Ever Made

May 2026 10 min read Watch History

Some watches tell time. Others tell history. The designs on this list belong to the second category — pieces that transcended their functional purpose to become cultural artifacts, design benchmarks, and investment-grade objects of desire. Compiled from horological significance, lasting influence, and cultural impact, these are the ten watch designs that every collector, enthusiast, and curious newcomer should understand.

01
Rolex Submariner
Rolex · Introduced 1953 · Swiss Mechanical

The Submariner is arguably the most copied watch design in history and the template for every dive watch that followed. Introduced in 1953 with a water resistance of 100 meters (later 200m, then 300m), it was designed as a professional diving instrument. What Rolex couldn't have anticipated was that the Submariner would become the definitive "dress in any situation" sports watch — equally at home at a board meeting as underwater. The Mercedes hands, rotating bezel, and Oyster case are so deeply embedded in watch culture that they feel like they were ordained rather than designed. Retail today exceeds $10,000. Secondary market prices are considerably higher for vintage references.

02
Omega Speedmaster Professional
Omega · Introduced 1957 · Mechanical Chronograph

NASA selected the Speedmaster Professional for all manned space missions in 1965, and it became the first watch worn on the moon in July 1969 on Neil Armstrong's wrist. No other watch can claim that. The "Moonwatch" — with its hesalite crystal, tachymeter bezel, and Calibre 321 movement (in original references) — remains in continuous production with essentially the same design as its 1957 introduction. Omega has resisted the temptation to modernize the Moonwatch excessively, understanding that its design integrity is its most valuable asset.

03
Audemars Piguet Royal Oak
AP · Introduced 1972 · Gérald Genta Design

Designed overnight by Gérald Genta for the 1972 Basel Watch Fair, the Royal Oak was the world's first luxury sports watch — and the first to command a luxury price in stainless steel, then considered a humble material. Its octagonal bezel with exposed screws, integrated bracelet, and "Petite Tapisserie" dial were revolutionary departures from watch design norms of the era. Today the Royal Oak Selfwinding 15202 is one of the most difficult watches to purchase at retail, with years-long waitlists and secondary market premiums exceeding 300% of retail on certain references.

04
Patek Philippe Nautilus
Patek Philippe · Introduced 1976 · Gérald Genta Design

Also designed by Genta — commissioned by Patek after the Royal Oak's success — the Nautilus has become the most financially significant sports watch of the modern era. The Ref. 5711, discontinued in 2021 after Thierry Stern's announcement it would cease production, now trades on secondary markets for $150,000–$200,000 for a watch that retailed at approximately $34,000. Its porthole-inspired case, horizontal lines dial, and integrated bracelet remain as striking today as in 1976.

05
Cartier Tank
Cartier · Introduced 1917 · Louis Cartier Design

Louis Cartier designed the Tank in 1917, inspired by the Renault FT tanks he saw on the Western Front of World War I. The rectangular case with side rails evoking tank treads was unlike anything produced before. Over a century later, the Tank remains in production — worn by presidents, royalty, and cultural icons across every generation. It is the definitive dress watch, the reference point for rectangular case design, and proof that a truly great design requires no evolutionary update to remain relevant.

06
Rolex Daytona
Rolex · Introduced 1963 · Racing Chronograph

Named for the Daytona International Speedway, the Daytona was designed for professional racing drivers. The Paul Newman Daytona references — named after the actor who wore his Ref. 6239 for decades — sold at Phillips auction in 2017 for $17.75 million, setting a world record for any wristwatch at auction at that time. The modern Daytona remains one of Rolex's hardest watches to buy at retail, with demand dramatically exceeding supply every year since the 1980s.

07
IWC Portugieser
IWC · Introduced 1939 · Portuguese Market Commission

Originally produced for Portuguese merchants who requested a pocket watch-grade movement in a wristwatch case, the Portugieser's large 42mm case (enormous for its era), clean dial, and Arabic numerals became the defining characteristics of a lineage IWC has maintained for over 80 years. Its influence on "pure" watch design — uncluttered, proportionally precise — is immeasurable.

08
Jaeger-LeCoultre Reverso
JLC · Introduced 1931 · Art Deco Masterpiece

The Reverso was created in 1931 for British officers playing polo in India who needed a watch whose crystal could be protected during play. The reversible rectangular case — which could flip to protect the crystal — is one of the most ingenious mechanical solutions in watch history. The caseback became a canvas for personalization: engraving, enameling, and miniature painting. Art Deco aesthetics have rarely been expressed more purely in mechanical form.

09
Seiko SKX007
Seiko · Introduced 1996 · Japanese Automatic

No list of iconic designs is complete without acknowledging the watch that democratized automatic watchmaking for an entire generation. The SKX007 offered a reliable, fully automatic movement, 200m water resistance, and handsome sports watch aesthetics at sub-$200 pricing. Discontinued in 2019, it now trades at a premium on secondary markets — a testament to its design perfection relative to its market position. The SKX is the gateway watch for millions of collectors worldwide.

10
Casio G-Shock DW-5600
Casio · Introduced 1983 · Kikuo Ibe Design

Kikuo Ibe dropped a watch accidentally and decided to build one that could survive being dropped from any height. The result was the G-Shock — which passed 123 prototype iterations before the DW-5000C shipped in 1983. The squared case, solar power, multi-band atomic timekeeping, and absolute shock and water resistance created a new category. Today the G-Shock is the best-selling watch in history, crossing 100 million units. Its cultural reach spans military use, streetwear, and high fashion collaborations. It is function distilled to design.
