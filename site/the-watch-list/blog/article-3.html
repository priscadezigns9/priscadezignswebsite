How to Choose Your First Watch — A Practical Guide

Buying your first serious watch is more satisfying — and more permanent — than most purchases. Done right, you'll wear it for decades. Here's a practical guide to getting it right.

Set a Realistic Budget

Under $200: you can get an excellent Japanese automatic (Seiko, Orient) or a Swiss quartz. $200-500: mechanical options improve significantly. Over $500: Swiss automatics, sapphire crystals, and better finishing become standard.

Movement Type

Quartz: battery-powered, extremely accurate, low maintenance. Automatic: self-winding, mechanical, no battery, requires service every 3-5 years. Solar: battery-free, powered by light, near-perfect accuracy.

Case Size

The average wrist suits a 38-42mm case diameter. Larger doesn't mean more luxurious — a perfectly proportioned 38mm dress watch looks more considered than an oversized 46mm. Try watches on before buying if possible.

Water Resistance

30m: splash resistant only (not for swimming). 50m: suitable for swimming. 100m: snorkelling. 200m+: diving. Daily wear watch should be at least 50m.

Crystal Type

Acrylic (plastic): scratches easily but polishes. Mineral glass: scratch-resistant, cheaper. Sapphire: extremely scratch-resistant, premium watches only. Sapphire is worth seeking even at lower price points.

Strap vs Bracelet

Metal bracelets are dressier and more durable. Leather straps are classic but require care. NATO straps (nylon) are casual and virtually indestructible. Most watches can accept aftermarket straps — this extends versatility enormously.
