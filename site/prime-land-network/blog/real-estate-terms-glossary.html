Real Estate Terms Every Buyer Should Know

The first time you sit across from a real estate agent or mortgage broker, the terminology can feel designed to overwhelm. It isn't — but you do need to know it. Here's a plain-English glossary of every term that will come up in your transaction.

Financing Terms

Pre-qualification: a rough estimate of how much you might borrow based on self-reported information. Carries no weight in an offer. Pre-approval: a lender has reviewed your actual income, credit, and assets and committed to lending up to a specific amount. This is what sellers take seriously. LTV (Loan-to-Value Ratio): your loan amount divided by the property value. 80% LTV means 20% down payment. Below 80% avoids PMI. PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance): charged monthly when your LTV exceeds 80% — typically 0.5-1.5% of the loan annually. Eliminated when you reach 20% equity.

Offer and Contract Terms

Earnest money deposit: a good-faith deposit (typically 1-3% of purchase price) showing your intention to complete the transaction. Non-refundable if you walk away without a contingency. Contingencies: conditions that must be met for the sale to proceed. Common contingencies: inspection (right to exit if inspection reveals issues), financing (exit if you can't get the loan), and appraisal (exit if appraisal comes in below purchase price). Closing costs: the fees paid at settlement, typically 2-5% of the loan amount. Includes lender fees, title insurance, attorney fees, and escrow charges.

Property and Ownership Terms

Title: the legal right to own the property. Clear title means no liens or disputes. Abstract of title: the historical record of ownership. Chain of title: the history of all previous owners. Deed: the document that transfers legal ownership. Recorded: a deed is recorded at the county clerk's office, making the ownership change public record. Fee simple: the most complete form of property ownership — you own the land and structure outright, indefinitely.

Appraisal and Market Terms

Appraisal: an independent opinion of value performed by a licensed appraiser, required by lenders. If the appraisal comes in below the purchase price, you must renegotiate, pay the difference in cash, or walk away. Comps (comparables): recently sold properties used to establish market value. Days on market (DOM): how long a listing has been active. High DOM suggests overpricing or a problem with the property. Seller's market vs buyer's market: seller's market = more buyers than homes (prices rise, multiple offers common). Buyer's market = more homes than buyers (prices flatten, negotiating leverage shifts to the buyer).
