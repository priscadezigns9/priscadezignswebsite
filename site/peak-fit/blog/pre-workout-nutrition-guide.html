Pre-Workout Nutrition: What to Eat Before Training

May 2026 7 min read Sports Nutrition

What you eat before training has a direct, measurable impact on performance. This isn't marketing copy — it's physiology. The right pre-workout nutrition strategy elevates energy levels, delays fatigue, improves focus, and creates the hormonal environment most conducive to productive training. The wrong approach — or no approach at all — results in suboptimal sessions, quicker exhaustion, and recovery that takes longer than it should. Getting this right is one of the highest-leverage changes you can make without any additional training.

The Three Fuels: Carbohydrates, Protein, and Fat

Your muscles run on ATP (adenosine triphosphate), which is primarily produced from carbohydrates during high-intensity exercise. Carbohydrates are the dominant pre-workout fuel for any training that involves intensity above 65% of max heart rate — which includes essentially all resistance training and most cardio.

Stored glycogen (carbohydrate stored in muscle and liver) is your body's preferred fuel for hard training. When glycogen is depleted — either from a very long session, inadequate carbohydrate intake, or training in a fasted state — performance drops measurably. You feel heavy, weak, and unable to push intensity. Research consistently shows that pre-workout carbohydrate intake improves performance compared to fasted training for most people.

Protein before training raises plasma amino acid levels during the session, increasing the MPS stimulus from training. It also reduces muscle protein breakdown. 20–40g of protein before training is ideal, especially if you won't be eating again for several hours post-workout.

Fat is not a useful pre-workout fuel. It digests slowly, can cause GI discomfort during training, and provides no immediate performance benefit. Keep fat minimal in the meal or snack immediately before training.

Timing: The Two Windows

Pre-workout nutrition operates in two distinct time windows, each requiring different meal choices:

2–3 hours before training: This is the ideal window for a full mixed meal. You have time to digest properly, and carbohydrates are converted to available glucose and glycogen before training starts. A full meal here means you arrive at the gym fueled, not digesting. Target: 50–80g of carbohydrates, 25–40g of protein, minimal fat.

30–60 minutes before training: If you're training first thing in the morning or can't schedule a meal 2–3 hours ahead, a smaller, rapidly-digesting snack prevents fasting performance loss. Target: 30–50g of simple carbohydrates, 15–20g of easy-to-digest protein. Solid food at this window may cause GI distress during training.

Caffeine: The Evidence-Based Ergogenic

Caffeine is the most thoroughly studied and reliably effective ergogenic aid available. It works by blocking adenosine receptors in the brain — adenosine is the compound that creates feelings of fatigue and sleepiness as it accumulates during waking hours. Blocking its effect allows you to train at higher intensities for longer before the perception of fatigue forces you to reduce effort.

The evidence-based dose: 3–6mg per kilogram of bodyweight, consumed 30–60 minutes before training. For a 75kg person, that's 225–450mg — equivalent to 2–4 cups of coffee. Start at the lower end if you're caffeine-sensitive. Habitual users may need higher doses due to tolerance but should cycle off periodically (2 weeks without caffeine resets sensitivity meaningfully).

Sources: black coffee, green tea, and caffeine tablets all work equivalently. The pre-workout supplement industry has built billions in revenue primarily on caffeine with proprietary blends of mostly ineffective additional ingredients. Black coffee is equally effective at a fraction of the cost.

Hydration: The Underrated Performance Variable

Even mild dehydration (2% of bodyweight) measurably reduces strength, power, and endurance. A 75kg person losing 1.5kg of water weight through sweat has impaired performance — this is achievable in a single moderate workout in warm conditions. Starting training already dehydrated compounds the problem significantly.

Target: drink 500–600ml of water in the 2 hours before training. During training, 150–250ml every 15–20 minutes during intense sessions. After training, drink 1.5x the weight you lost in sweat (most people lose 0.5–1kg per hour of intense exercise). Sports drinks with electrolytes (sodium, potassium) are useful for sessions lasting over 60–90 minutes where sweat losses are significant; for shorter sessions, water is adequate.

What to Avoid

  • High-fat meals within 2 hours of training: Slows gastric emptying, causes heaviness and potential GI distress
  • Excessive fiber immediately pre-workout: Fermentable fiber can cause gas and cramping during exercise
  • Large food volume within 60 minutes of training: The body prioritizes digestion over muscular blood flow
  • Alcohol the night before important training: Impairs sleep quality, dehydrates, reduces next-day performance
  • Training fully fasted for heavy strength work: Acceptable for light cardio; performance on heavy compound lifts is measurably worse fasted

Sample Pre-Workout Meals

2-3 Hours Before Training
Full Mixed Meal

Chicken breast (150g) + white rice (200g cooked) + steamed broccoli. ~500 calories, 45g protein, 65g carbs, 8g fat. Clean, digestible, complete.

1-2 Hours Before Training
Moderate Snack

Greek yogurt (200g) + banana + small handful of oats. ~350 calories, 25g protein, 55g carbs. Easy to digest, stable energy.

30-60 Minutes Before Training
Quick Fuel

White toast with honey + whey protein shake. ~300 calories, 25g protein, 45g fast-digesting carbs. Minimal digestion burden.

Early Morning Training (No Time)
Minimal Pre-Training

Coffee + banana. Take training seriously post-workout. For strength sessions, at minimum consume something over pure fasting.
