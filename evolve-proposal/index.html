Section 02

Why Electric — Why Now

The global shift to electric vehicles is no longer a trend — it's an economic and operational reality. Organisations that act early lock in cost advantages and brand positioning that late movers cannot recover.

The Global Direction

The world is moving electric — and it is moving fast. Global EV sales surpassed 17 million units in 2024, representing over 20% of all new vehicle sales worldwide. Governments across Europe, Asia, and the Americas have committed to phasing out internal combustion engines, with targets ranging from 2030 to 2040. This is not a speculative future — it is already the present reality in most developed markets.

Electric vehicles offer a fundamental shift in how organisations think about fleet costs. With no oil changes, no transmission servicing, and up to 70% lower fuel costs, the total cost of ownership for an EV fleet is significantly lower than an equivalent petrol fleet over any three-year period. This is not marketing — it is documented by the US Department of Energy, Consumer Reports, and fleet operators across multiple industries globally.

For the Caribbean — and specifically for Trinidad and Tobago — the case is even stronger. With fuel prices subject to subsidy volatility and imported parts costs rising, locking in a low-maintenance, electrically-powered fleet is a strategic hedge that pays dividends from the first month of operation. The question is no longer whether to transition — it is how soon.

Common Concerns

Myths vs. Facts

It is normal for organisations to have questions before transitioning to an EV fleet. Here is what the data says.

"Chinese EVs are unreliable."
BYD is the world's largest EV manufacturer by volume, surpassing Tesla in global sales in 2023. GAC Aion has delivered over 1.5 million EVs worldwide. These are not experimental brands — they are the benchmark for mass-market EV reliability. JD Power and Euro NCAP data consistently rate BYD, GAC, Leapmotor, and Denza at or above equivalent Japanese and Korean competitors. Leapmotor earned a 5-star Euro NCAP rating in 2023. Denza is a joint venture with Mercedes-Benz — premium engineering at Caribbean pricing.
"What happens if the battery fails?"
Every EVOLVE vehicle carries an 8-year manufacturer warranty on the battery pack. Battery degradation across BYD, GAC, Leapmotor, and Denza fleets averages just 2.3% per year — meaning after 8 years, the battery still holds over 81% of its original capacity. Real-world fleet data from global operators confirms EV batteries consistently outlast their warranty periods.
"What if parts are not available locally?"
Genuine replacement parts are supplied directly by EVOLVE Mobility. Where a part is not currently available locally, EVOLVE coordinates sourcing on your behalf — no grey market, no unverified third-party suppliers. Contact EVOLVE directly to confirm stock and lead times for specific models. All servicing is carried out by SBCS University-trained technicians in-country.
"What if we need public charging infrastructure?"
Standard AC charging requires only a regular 240V outlet — the same type used for heavy-duty appliances. No specialised infrastructure is needed to get started. A fleet of 5 vehicles charging overnight adds approximately TT$100 to the nightly electricity bill — a fraction of daily petrol costs.

For corporate or public charging needs, EVOLVE Mobility currently operates 8 charging stations across Trinidad & Tobago, with more locations being added as the network expands. Station locations are available upon request — contact Prisca Dezigns directly for the current map. TTT vehicles can utilise this network for top-ups during operating hours, while overnight compound charging remains the primary and most cost-efficient method.
"EVs do not have enough range for our operations."
The BYD Atto 3 delivers up to 420 km per charge. The GAC Aion LX Plus reaches 1,008 km on a single charge under CLTC testing. Trinidad & Tobago is approximately 80 km end to end — a full charge covers an entire week of typical corporate driving without needing a top-up.
"EVs cannot handle Trinidad's roads."
EVOLVE's vehicle lineup is selected specifically for Caribbean road conditions. Models like the GAC Aion V and BYD Atto 3 feature high ground clearance, independent multi-link rear suspension, and reinforced underbody protection — built to handle uneven surfaces, potholes, and rural roads. These are not low-slung city cars. They are purpose-built SUVs and crossovers engineered for real-world terrain, tested across diverse emerging markets with road conditions comparable to T&T.
"EV maintenance is complicated and expensive."
EVs have up to 90% fewer moving parts than internal combustion vehicles. There is no oil, no transmission fluid, no spark plugs, no exhaust system. Global fleet operators report 40–60% lower maintenance costs compared to equivalent petrol fleets over a 5-year period.
"The Caribbean heat will damage the battery."
BYD's Blade Battery is engineered to operate normally in temperatures ranging from -35°C to 60°C. Trinidad's average ambient temperature of 28–34°C is well within the battery's safe operating range. When temperatures rise above 40°C, the vehicle's active liquid cooling system automatically activates — running coolant through the battery pack to maintain the ideal temperature range. This is the same technology used in BYD fleets deployed across Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and other tropical and desert climates far hotter than T&T. The battery is not exposed to open air — it is a sealed, thermally managed unit built to handle real-world heat.
"Are EVs actually quieter and smoother to drive?"
Significantly. Electric motors produce near-zero cabin noise — no engine rumble, no gear shifts, no exhaust vibration. Acceleration is instant and completely smooth from a standstill. For a corporate fleet carrying executives, staff, and media personnel, this is a meaningful upgrade in comfort and professionalism. GAC Aion, Denza, and Leapmotor models are engineered with premium NVH (Noise, Vibration, Harshness) insulation — delivering a ride quality that rivals vehicles costing twice the price. Once TTT's team experiences the difference, there is no going back.
"EVs are more likely to catch fire."
This is one of the most common misconceptions — and the data says the opposite. According to global vehicle fire statistics, electric vehicles experience approximately 25 fires per 100,000 vehicles sold. Petrol and diesel vehicles experience 1,530 fires per 100,000 — over 60 times higher. Hybrid vehicles are the highest risk category at 3,475 fires per 100,000. The vehicle fires making headlines in Trinidad are overwhelmingly petrol-powered. EVs have no fuel tank, no combustible liquid, and no hot exhaust system — significantly reducing the ignition risk that causes most roadside fires.
"Charging means stopping at a public station — that's a security risk."
This is actually one of the strongest safety advantages of EVs for TTT's fleet. Your compound charger is installed on your own premises. Vehicles charge overnight — or any time they are parked and not in use — in a secure, controlled environment. There is no need to stop at a public gas station at any hour, eliminating the exposure risk that comes with roadside fuel stops. Every morning, every vehicle in the fleet leaves fully charged, fully safe — without a single driver having to step out at a gas station. For a corporate fleet operating across T&T, this is a significant operational and personal security upgrade.
